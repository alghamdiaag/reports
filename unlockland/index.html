Executive Summary

The Insight in Three Lines

Real estate development teams face one problem that appears in different forms across every project: decisions are made on disconnected parts of the project, not on the project as a whole. Zoning sits in one tool, finance in Excel, feasibility with a consultant, and the committee reviews documents that do not speak to each other.


This problem peaks in Mixed-Use and Commercial projects — where variables interact and stakeholders multiply. It is still largely unsolved in the market today.


UnlockLand has the product closest to solving it. The question is not what to build — but who to target first and with what message.

Job Map

Eight Moments a Feasibility Analyst Lives Through

Core job: evaluate the feasibility of developing a real estate site and make an investment decision in light of planning requirements and target financial returns.

Layer 1 — Narrative

Eight Moments Where the Market Fails

What the feasibility analyst experiences at each step — in commercial language, without research jargon.

① Define — Before the Start

The Moment of Choosing the Target

When an analyst is assigned to study a new market, they face a question no tool answers: Where do we start? Is this market even suitable?

Today, this decision is built on personal experience or a consulting report that takes weeks. There is no unified framework for scoring the attractiveness of different asset classes with one consistent methodology.

The team that can evaluate three markets in one day gains a decision advantage competitors do not have.
② Locate — Data Collection

When Weeks Become a Bottleneck

For every new site, the team collects zoning data, rental rates, construction costs, and compliance rules. Each source uses a different language and logic.

In an unfamiliar market, this phase takes 3 to 6 weeks. During that time, the opportunity may be lost.

Whoever provides “decision-ready data,” not “raw data,” saves time money cannot replace.
③ Prepare — Build the Structure

The Conflicting Assumptions Problem

This is the most wasteful moment: the development team and the finance team are not working from the same numbers. This conflict is often discovered only in the committee meeting.

The developer calculates based on construction cost X, while the financial analyst inputs Y. Zoning allows one density, while the financial model assumes a higher density.

One unified assumptions model that everyone works from is not a luxury; it is an operating necessity.
④ Confirm — Verification

Late Discoveries That Cost

“Why didn’t we know this earlier?” — this question appears in almost every project. Discovering conflicts late resets the project clock from zero.

Verification depends on external consultants, and each consultant speaks from a separate logic.

Verification that happens in hours, not weeks, changes the nature of the decision itself.
⑤ Execute — Feasibility Evaluation

The Most Wasteful Moment of Truth

The team usually builds one scenario and then edits it manually. In Mixed-Use, one decision change means recalculating three models across three different files.

The reason is not laziness — it is that building an alternative scenario often means rebuilding the Excel model from scratch.

The team that evaluates 10 scenarios while a competitor evaluates two makes better decisions systematically.
⑥ Monitor — Monitoring

Old Information That Creates Losses

In markets such as Saudi Arabia and the UAE, construction costs and rental rates change in ways that can make a feasibility model from three months ago outdated.

The team may not know the model is outdated until the deal is confirmed on numbers that no longer reflect reality.

A system that alerts the team when a critical variable changes turns risk from “invisible” into “manageable.”
⑦ Modify — Modification

The Missing Version Control

When a major assumption changes, the team starts building a new version of the model. The old version is not deleted, but it gets lost. Two weeks later, no one remembers “which Excel is the correct one.”

A system that saves every version, shows who changed what and when, and allows rollback with one click.
⑧ Conclude — Decision Closure

The Moment That Wins or Breaks the Deal

Investment committees do not read Excel models. If the memo is not clear and backed by a full audit trail, the first question becomes: “Where did this number come from?”

Building an IC Memo manually takes one to three days. Any error in transferring numbers damages credibility.

An IC Memo generated directly from the model turns the committee meeting from an interrogation into a decision.
